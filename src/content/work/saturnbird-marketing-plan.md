## The brief

Saturnbird had product–market fit at home and a strong instinct that the same proposition would resonate in North America — but no proof of it yet, and a budget that would only stretch far enough to be wrong once.

The brief was to design the **smallest possible market test** that would tell us whether to commit, and to plan the follow-on spend on the assumption that the test would tell us *something useful either way*.

## The approach

The plan was structured in two phases with a hard checkpoint between them.

### Phase one — validate

- Tightly geo-targeted paid social in two metro markets
- One landing page, three creative directions, identical CTAs
- A clear definition of "this worked" set in writing before the buy

### Phase two — commit (conditional)

- Predefined creative and channel mix triggered only if Phase 1 hit specific thresholds
- A defined "no, redirect budget" path with equal seriousness — usually the missing half of expansion plans

The work that mattered most was *not the campaign design*. It was getting the leadership team to agree, in advance and in writing, on what "the test failed" would mean. That clarity is what turns a marketing budget into a decision.

## What it produced

A clean go/no-go framework, three creative directions ready to run, and a leadership team aligned on what success and failure each looked like before any money was spent.

## What I'd do again

Always force a written definition of failure before the campaign runs. It changes how everyone — including marketing — interprets the data afterward.
